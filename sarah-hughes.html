Sarah Hughes Biography

Sarah Hughes Skating

Hughes began skating at the age of three. Robin Wagner who also choreographed for her from 1994 finally became her head coach in January 1998. In the 1997 to 1998 season, Sarah won the junior title at the 1998 U.S. Championships. She later completed on the ISU Junior Grand Prix the following season and won the silver medal at the 1998 to 1999 Junior Grand Prix Final.

She then took silver at the 1999 World Junior Championships held in November 1998. Hughes made her Grand Prix debut, winning the bronze medal at the 1999 Trophée Lalique in the 1999 to 2000 season. She also won the bronze medal at the 2000 U.S. Championships and was credited with a triple-salchow-triple-loop combination.

Moreover, Hughes placed 5th at the 2000 World Championships. She won three medals on the Grand Prix circuit and won the bronze medal at the 2000 to 2001 Grand Prix of Figure Skating Final in the 2001 to 2002 season.

Sarah Hughes Olympic Win

Hughes placed fourth in the short program after being penalized for underrotating her triple flip and lutz at the 2002 Olympics. She landed seven triple jumps, including a triple toe loop-triple loop and a triple salchow-triple loop combination in her long program. She went ahead to win the long program like the three contenders ahead of her all later made mistakes in their respective long programs.

According to figure skating rules at that time, is one placed fourth in the short program, but won the free skate, they could not automatically win the event. Retired figure skater Michelle Kwan was in first place after the short program and she would have to lose the free program to Hughes and one other skater as well. Furthermore, she won the free skate where Irina Slutskaya placed second in that portion, ahead of Kwan.

Hughes went ahead to win the gold medal at the age of 16 years and 295 days. She became the youngest figure skating Olympic champion. Hughes is also the only American woman to have won the Olympic title without ever having won either a World or U.S. senior national title.
